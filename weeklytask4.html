A Fitness Mobile Applications ‘Twazon’

 

Twazon is a Saudi Arabic health and fitness app, designed by academics to combat obesity in Saudi Arabia. The app tracks calorie intake and exercise as well as providing educational information to encourage healthy eating and behaviour. The main feature setting the app apart from any other is that it draws all of its food plans and recommendations from a database of Saudi foods.

 

In researching usability testing of this app a variety of literature was found and used to form the basis of the following analysis, the first article that was examined was about the development of the app including the evidence that the app is based on and employs and the features, layout and operation of the app. Usability was also tested and documented in this article. A second article was also referenced this article detailed more rigorous independent usability testing of the Twazon app using a range of different metrics.

 

The user experience of the Twazon app is predominantly negative as see in the results of [1] but what attributes have led to this?

 

The Twazon app development focused on access to a Saudi-food based meal planner and following their evidence-based weight loss practice. The developers outline just how they intended to implement this throughout [2]. Educational information was to be presented in the app and the quality of information and that information’s presentation is reassured in their user testing [2].

Part of their testing saw experts in the health and nutrition industry use the app for a week and provide feedback. This testing was very good to validate the heath information however no focus of this was on the usability or user experience. This highlights a fundamental flaw in their approach to the development of the app, they used good information, but they focused only on providing access to that information and overlooked how the information was accessed and its use.

 

The independent usability testing detailed in [1] use seven measurable metrics to further pin down the shortcomings of the Twazon app. The metrics used are; effectiveness, efficiency, satisfaction, memorability, errors, learnability and cognitive load all of these metrics are intrinsic to the use of the app and can provide valuable information in assessing the user experience. The results of testing across these metrics showed poor results for most participants. The most telling result is for cognitive load and this show a general negative UX. To evaluate this the participants were given a questionnaire with questions about; mental demand of the task, physical demand, time pressure they felt, effort(how hard it was to complete), performance(their own performance) and frustration level [1]. Many of these attributes provide valuable experience information. Figure 1 from [1] below shows the results of the testing. Across the attributes mental demand, temporal demand effort and frustration are all quite high of these temporal demand and frustration are attributes commonly associated with a negative UX [1]. High scores in mental demand and effort also point towards a negative UX as users should not have think very much about the operation of the app. Overall the independent study found the “Twazon app has and low level of usability” [1]

The Twazon apps developers did conduct a second style of user testing however it did not consider the usability attributes and factors as the independent testing did. The developers testing also did not use measurable metrics instead just asking the participants for questions and collecting qualitative data in the form of verbal feedback. They also did not iterate in their testing phase having just the two different groups try the single version of software. In all this again highlight what led to the poor UX of this app, the apps development focused on the functionality of the system rather than being developed with the attributes of usability strongly considered. A more human centred approach would have likely improved the user experience.

 

The variety of source material gave unique insight to the product especially its conception and implementation. Using the developers article helped grasp what their intentions and their goals were for the product, it showed where they could have adjusted their implementation to produce a better experience for the end user. One unique perspective that this article allowed was to see some of the mindset and misaligned focus that likely led to the apps negative UX. “Moreover, the majority [of participants] felt that the app needed to be even easier and should offer a detailed explanation on how to use the app.” [2] this is excerpted from the future suggestions paragraph of the user testing. From this you can see that the developers understanding of the testing is limited as the participants have said that it needs to be easier to use and the developers have assumed it is already easy to use. As the developer of course it will be easy for them to use. This is further reinforced in later quotes “The results show that the usability of the app varies between potential users and this might be attributed to the different learning styles or technological literacy of users.” [2]

The use of the independent testing was also highly valuable as it supplied a stark contrast between comprehensive usability testing and the very limited testing in [2]
